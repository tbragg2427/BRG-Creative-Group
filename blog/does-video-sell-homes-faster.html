Does Video Actually Sell Homes Faster? What the Data Says

By Trevor Bragg · BRG Creative Group · July 2026

In the listing video vs photos debate, video wins on attention: homes marketed with video draw more inquiries, hold buyers on the listing longer, and produce better-qualified showings than photos alone, and that attention tends to translate into faster sales. The honest caveat is that the most famous statistics behind the claim are older and thinner than most marketing decks admit. This article separates what the data actually supports from what gets recycled, and shows where video earns its cost.

Where do the famous video statistics come from?

If you have sat through a listing presentation or a media company's pitch, you have probably heard that listings with video receive 403 percent more inquiries than listings without. It is the most quoted number in real estate video, and almost nobody who quotes it knows where it came from. The trail leads back to the Australian property portal Domain, in the early 2010s, reporting on inquiry rates across its own listings. One market, one platform, and more than a decade ago.

That does not make the number false. It makes it a snapshot, not a law. The same goes for most of the statistics that circulate in this debate: figures about faster sales and higher prices get passed from blog to blog until the original source is impossible to pin down, and the studies behind them rarely control for the obvious confounder, which is that the agents and sellers who invest in video also tend to price well, stage well, and market harder across the board.

So we will not hang this argument on a single recycled percentage. The better question is what the evidence, taken together, consistently shows.

So does video actually help homes sell faster?

Directionally, yes, and the pattern is consistent across markets and years even where the exact magnitudes wobble. Three things show up again and again:

  • Video listings earn more attention. Portal data and platform studies have repeatedly found higher inquiry and engagement rates on listings with video. Buyers stop, watch, and stay on the page longer. Attention is the raw material every sale is made from.
  • Video pre-qualifies the showing. A buyer who has walked the home on screen already understands the layout and the flow. The people who book a showing after watching a film are further along than the people who swiped through twelve photos. Fewer wasted showings, more serious ones, faster path to an offer.
  • Buyers say they want it. Buyer surveys, including the National Association of Realtors' research on how buyers use listing media, consistently rank video and virtual tours among the most useful features on a listing. Demand for the format is not in question.

What video cannot do is rescue a mispriced home. Days on market is driven first by price, condition, and inventory. A cinematic film on an overpriced listing produces a beautifully documented sit. Video accelerates a sale that is priced to happen; it does not manufacture one.

The defensible claim is this: video reliably increases attention and inquiry quality, and on a well-priced home, that added attention is exactly what shortens the timeline.

Listing video vs photos: when does each one win?

Photos are the baseline everywhere. They power the portal listing, and no video replaces a sharp, well-lit photo set. The real question is when video is worth adding on top, and the answer follows the money in the listing.

Video earns its cost fastest where lifestyle is what the buyer is actually purchasing. Waterfront, golf course, new construction, acreage, and anything where the setting and the feel justify the price. A photo can show a dock; a film shows the morning a buyer will spend on it. That is the same experience-first principle we wrote about in our yacht charter marketing guide: when the product is a feeling, only motion can rehearse it.

Video also pulls hardest in crowded segments, where thirty similar listings compete for the same buyer pool and the one with a film is the one that gets shared, and on out-of-area buyers, who rely on media to shortlist homes before they ever fly in. South Florida sees more of those buyers than almost any market in the country.

Where can photos carry the load alone? A well-priced entry-level condo in a fast-moving segment will often sell on strong photography before a video could even be edited. Spending on video there is a branding decision for the agent, not a requirement of the sale.

What separates a listing video that sells from one that just exists?

Plenty of listing videos are just photo slideshows with music, and they perform like it. The films that move buyers share a few traits:

  • They tell the day, not the floor plan. Sunrise on the terrace, coffee in the kitchen, the walk to the water. The floor plan is in the photos and the MLS; the film's job is emotion.
  • They are cut for each platform. A vertical edit under a minute for Instagram and TikTok, and a longer film for YouTube, email, and the listing page. One shoot, two jobs.
  • They keep people in frame. Empty rooms photograph beautifully and convert quietly. A person moving through the space gives the buyer someone to be.
  • They get distributed, not just uploaded. A film that sits on the MLS link did ten percent of its job. Reels, targeted ads, agent channels, and email do the rest.

Should you invest in video or better photos first?

Photos first, always. If the photography is weak, fix that before anything else, because every channel from the portal to the postcard runs on it. Once the photo set is strong, video is the multiplier, and it multiplies twice.

The first return is on the listing itself: more attention, better showings, a stronger position in the segment. The second return belongs to the agent. Every film becomes proof in the next listing presentation, and sellers increasingly choose the agent who can show a marketing machine over the one who promises a sign in the yard. The listing sells, and the film keeps recruiting the next one. That compounding effect is the quiet reason top teams budget for video as a standing line item rather than a listing-by-listing luxury.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a real estate listing video cost?

Costs range from a few hundred dollars for a simple walkthrough to a few thousand for a cinematic film with drone aerials, licensed music, and social cuts. The right spend scales with the price point of the home: the higher the commission at stake, the easier the video pays for itself.

Do lower-priced listings need video too?

Not always. Sharp professional photography is the non-negotiable baseline for every listing. Video earns its cost fastest on homes where lifestyle drives the price, on listings that need to stand out in a crowded segment, and for agents using each film to win the next listing appointment.

How long should a listing video be?

Under a minute for Instagram and TikTok, where the goal is to stop the scroll and drive profile visits. One to three minutes for YouTube, the MLS link, and email, where a serious buyer is willing to sit with the property. One shoot should produce both cuts.

Does video help a listing show up in search and AI tools?

Yes. A listing film published on YouTube is searchable content that outlives the sale, and pages that hold a visitor's attention longer tend to perform better in search. Video also gives AI assistants and portals richer material to draw from when buyers research a neighborhood or an agent.
